1. What is the tax tribunal

The Ministry of Justice (MOJ) operates the tax tribunal service.

The tax tribunal’s decision-making authority is completely independent of government. A judge gives an impartial ruling on every case.

You can appeal most tax decisions

You should consider resolving your dispute before you submit an appeal. For certain disputes, you will need to ask for your case to be looked into before you can take your case to the tax tribunal.

You can appeal against:

  • decisions made by HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) such as tax owed or penalties for late filing or payment
  • seizure of goods by HMRC or UK Border Force
  • enquiries into tax returns or requests for information by HMRC or the National Crime Agency (NCA)

There’s a different way to appeal decisions about tax credits and Council Tax.

The appeal process depends on your case

Your appeal or application will be considered by the First-tier Tribunal (Tax). You should prepare your case carefully before you submit your appeal.

Warning You will need to submit the correct documents and pay an initial fee for your appeal to proceed.

The tax tribunal will process your case and let you know whether your case needs further documents or a hearing.

Your case will be looked at by a judge, either on paper or at a hearing, which you can attend in person. The judge will consider both sides in the dispute before making an impartial decision.

Straightforward fixed penalty disputes can be decided within 3 months with no need for a hearing. Complex cases involving principles of law can sometimes last for 2 years or more.

Legislation

The tribunal must follow the Tribunal Procedure (First-tier Tribunal) (Tax Chamber) Rules 2009.

Read the practice directions and practice statements for more detailed guidance.

The tribunal was set up by the Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007 and Transfer of Tribunal Functions and Revenue and Customs Appeals Order 2009.
